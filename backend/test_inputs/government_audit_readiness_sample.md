# Enterprise Directive — Government Client Engagement Controls (Internal Draft)

## Purpose

This directive establishes minimum expectations for teams supporting public-sector and regulated government clients. It is intended to harmonize disclosure discipline, decision approvals, and evidence retention ahead of external scrutiny.

## Decision approvals

All commitments priced above five hundred thousand dollars require written approval from the regional delivery director. Email confirmations are acceptable when forwarded into the deal room repository within forty-eight hours. Verbal sign-offs may be noted in the weekly steering slide deck but do not replace repository evidence.

For surge engagements labeled “audit-sensitive,” account leads must obtain an additional countersignature from the Enterprise Risk liaison. The liaison roster is maintained informally by the chief of staff and updated via quarterly email rather than a controlled directory.

## Government-client handling

Teams must classify engagements against the government-client taxonomy within the CRM. Where taxonomy codes conflict with procurement labels supplied by the client, account leads should “use reasonable judgment” and document decisions in the shared engagement spreadsheet until the taxonomy council publishes clarifications.

Client-facing narratives describing data residency and subprocessors must be reviewed before publication. Review ownership rotates among available senior counsel; no secondary reviewer is named when primary counsel is on leave.

## Disclosure rules

Material developments affecting pricing, scope, or regulatory posture must be disclosed to government procurement contacts without undue delay. “Undue delay” is interpreted locally by the deal team in consultation with account counsel when available.

Retention copies of outbound disclosures are saved to the client folder when practical. Historical disclosures prior to fiscal year 2023 remain on archived laptops that are not indexed by the enterprise search appliance.

## Evidence retention

Critical approvals, conflict attestations, and delegation letters must be retained for seven years. Teams largely rely on attachment folders inside the collaboration workspace; there is no enforced legal hold workflow tied to engagement closure.

Quarterly spot checks are performed by internal audit on a sample basis. Findings are summarized verbally to segment leadership; formal remediation tickets are optional when leadership agrees the exposure is contained.

## Escalation

Escalations for audit inquiries land with the account lead first. If the account lead is unavailable, coverage defaults to the most senior seller attached to the opportunity — there is no documented backup approver matrix for audit-response ownership after hours.

For regulator-directed evidence pulls with less than twenty-four hours’ notice, teams assemble artifacts using the “rapid binder” checklist stored on a shared drive tab. Two required checklist items — named escalation owner and indexed exhibit list — are marked “TBD pending tooling.”

## Operational gaps visible in this draft

Decision approvals depend on informal liaison rosters and forward chains that may be incomplete. Disclosure timing hinges on local judgment without a clocked SLA. Evidence lives in workspace folders without immutable indexing. Audit escalation lacks a named backup owner and a rehearsed handoff path when leadership travels.

## Reporting and metrics

Segment dashboards highlight pipeline health and forecast accuracy. Controls-adjacent metrics — disclosure latency distributions, approval completeness rates, conflict attestation coverage — appear only in quarterly business reviews and are not wired to operational alerting.

When metrics disagree between finance and delivery sources, reconciliation meetings resolve discrepancies verbally; durable reconciliation packets are optional unless audit requests them explicitly.

## Training and awareness

Annual training modules cover ethics and client confidentiality. Completion tracking gates expense submissions for some roles but not for extended contractor populations engaged on surge statements of work.

Localized government-client nuances are communicated through brown-bag sessions that are recorded inconsistently; newer hires sometimes rely on informal mentoring transcripts stored in chat histories outside records policies.
